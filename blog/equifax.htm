Equifax data breach.

Introduction.

In this report we will be exploring the Equifax data breach which occurred in September 2017.

Equifax is a financial information company that offers financial and credit reports of businesses and individuals. The company organizes, assimilates and analyzes data on more than 820 million consumers and more than 91 million businesses worldwide, and its database includes employee data contributed from more than 7,100 employers[2].

Equifax blamed the data breach on an apache struts vulnerability which allowed remote code execution on the servers holding their confidential data, this vulnerability is known as CVE-2017-5638 [3].

Because of the breach Equifax saw a loss of trust from their customers which led to a decrease in revenue. Equifax also saw the value of their stock fall[4].

Equifax was criticized for failing to act quickly enough as they had been made explicitly aware of the vulnerability that caused the data breach two months before it happened[5].

Since the breach happened very little has been done as far as making sure something like this doesn’t happen again beyond patching the initial vulnerability, more needs to be done by Equifax and the industry at large as well as stricter data protection laws from the United States especially concerning notification of effected people[6].

We will look at these points in more depth under individual headings.

Outline of data breach.

Equifax blamed the data breach on a vulnerability in apache struts known as the CVE-2017-5638 that was detected march of 2017, the vulnerability allowed for remote code execution which made it possible for the attackers to get the information of an estimated 145million people including names, birthdates, street addresses, credit card numbers, and Social Security numbers[7].

Apache struts is an open source model view controller framework aimed at web developers who are familiar with java and java servlets as well as the web development stack such as JavaScript, ajax, xml and soap[8].  

The vulnerability worked by allowing the attacking party to execute the malicious code on the server by making an incorrect http request with the malicious code placed in a spoofed content type this causes an exception error. When the exception error is thrown instead of printing the content type it instead executes it[9].

Effect on the organization.

The breach effected the company financially alongside its reputation and standing. Several key members of Equifax’s staff also resigned because of the breach.

Equifax has said they estimate to lose about 75million in revenue this year as a result of customers deferring business due to a lack of trust after the data breach, it is currently un clear if the this is just a blip on Equifax’s record the company seems to be moving in an upward trajectory[11].

Equifax shares fell drastically after the breach and have settled at a reduction of about 25% this is the most obvious marker of how the attack affected them. The graph below illustrates the fluctuation of their share price over the last year. The lowest point on the graph is September 11th just 4 days after Equifax announced the breach.

There have been a number of resignations by prominent Equifax staff members following the breach such as CEO Richard Smith, chief information officer David Webb will step down immediately and be replaced by Mark Rohrwasser, who was in charge of Equifax International IT operations since coming to the company in 2016 and chief security officer Susan Mauldin is stepping down as well[12].

Mistakes made.

The biggest mistake Equifax obviously made was not securing their systems that were created in the vulnerable apache struts software release, this breach could have been avoided if the company had put more resources into their cyber security. An anonymous security researcher told the press that they had warned Equifax of the breach months before it took place as the vulnerability had been in the wild since march of 2017 it is also noted that the department of homeland security also made Equifax aware of the bug[13].

Three of Equifax’s executives sold stock in the days after the breach occurred totaling to an amount of $1.8million, they claimed to have not known about the breach at the time of selling, however if it can be proven that they did know it would seem that Equifax executives took advantage of not being legally forced to disclose that they were victim to a security breach in order to make money[14][15].

Improvements (to be) made.

As the breach only occurred in the last year as of writing this report in March 2018. It is difficult to determine what Equifax has done to bolster their cyber security efforts. We will instead focus on what lessons can be learned from the breach and what improvements should be made.

Patch cycles need to be shortened, a patch cycle in this case would run through the currently implemented software across the business and check that any vulnerabilities that have been discovered since the software was created are found and removed. The rate at which this should be done has increased in the recent years as the rate of software releases increase[16].

External third-party testing of the application needs to be carried out more often. Penetration tests as they are called are necessary and would find likely have found this type of vulnerability. A system of Continual Vulnerability Assessment [17] is needed to check for known vulnerabilities, this kind of system would set up a framework for testing that no new vulnerabilities have been seen for the software. This would most likely have turned up the vulnerability after it had been released by apache.

Asset based risk assessment, by putting a value associated with the risk of the secured data being breached it would become easier for the company to increase their priority for cyber security, if Equifax had known the end cost of not securing their system well enough they would have put more resources into mitigating the chances as much as possible[16].

Changes also need to be made at the legislative level, we can see that in comparison to the European union’s strict 72hours to notify customers of a data breach, the united states give no time frame for its companies[18]. Laws such as these are starting to be created in the fallout of the data breach[19].

Conclusion.

The attack on Equifax’s system acted as a stark warning to the rest of the industry and consumers of how important it is to secure software systems especially those which allow access to sensitive information. Equifax is now paying for a lack of foresight and it will be carried with their company for years to come.

We can see from this report that Equifax did not take the securing of the sensitive consumer data that they were responsible for seriously enough or were ignorant of how important it was to keep this data secure as they have made so many people prime targets for cybercrimes such as identity theft and phishing attempts.

Companies must follow a strict regime of patch cycles, third party penetration testing and asset-based risk assessment, it is vital that companies invest more in securing their software systems from outside threats and vulnerabilities that occur over time.

The government must play a role in all jurisdictions they control. This it is important because the actions or inactions of the companies within their domain will and have affected the public both within and without the country.
